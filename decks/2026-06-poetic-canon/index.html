Mapping the Canon: Quantitative Approaches to Literary History · Darmstadt · June 18–19, 2026

Evolution of the Poetic Canon in the Princeton Prosody Archive

Wouter Haverals

Princeton University · Center for Digital Humanities

  • The question: how does a poetic canon change across more than two centuries of writing about poetry — in manuals and handbooks made to explain verse, meter, and prosody?
  • My angle — canonicity as usefulness in a teaching context; not poets explicitly selected or praised, but the ones reached for because they serve a purpose — the example you need when explaining how verse works.

01

Canonicity through use

  • “ So: canonicity through use” — a poet earns a place in a poetry manual; can be quoted, taught, and repurposed.
  • I will illustrate what that means with one concrete example.
  • This is Thomas Gray's Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard — first published 1751; shown here in a beautifully illustrated 1766 edition.
  • Gray's Elegy is one of the most popular poems in the English tradition — here it is printed whole, a poem meant to be read in full.
  • This is almost never how poem turns up in the works I study.

Gray’s Elegy, in pieces

Rarely the whole poem—usually a line someone can teach with.

Authority made through use—lines cut, copied, and repurposed.

“le jeu du découpage et du collage” Compagnon, 1979

  • It quickly became a stock example in poetry and versification handbooks — books that reflect on poetic form and instruct people about it.
  • Lines have been pressed into all kinds of lessons: about elocution; the musicality of language; plagiarism; rural themes in English poetry; scansion; even the use of adjectives.
  • None of these uses show the poem whole.
  • The poem becomes a scrapyard of lines — sometimes mutilated, lifted from their frame, repurposed for teaching.
  • This is what Antoine Compagnon’s calls “the game of cutting and grafting” (le jeu du découpage et du collage).

  • The Princeton Prosody Archive (or PPA for short) collects four centuries of books that teach how verse sounds, scans, and should be read aloud. These are poetry manuals, elocution handbooks, books about meter, versification.
  • All in all, the PPA contains around seven thousand works, two million pages, all full text.
  • That little graph shows the number of pages per year in this archive.
  • There are relatively few works in the 16th century and 17th century, but the number of works and thus pages increases rapidly in the 18th century.

    • 03

    Canonicity traced in the PPA

  • Now, how to actually trace a poetic canon in this material?

    • An archive of use

    Not an anthology of great literature—books that make verse legible, audible, and teachable.

    Poets as instruments. Names invoked to settle a rule—which poets become usable, repeatable common ground when writers explain how verse works.

    Their lines. Lines quoted to make a point concrete—cut into couplets and stanzas, made to do explanatory work, and able to travel without their poems.

  • As I said, I think of this as a canon of use. How does that show up in the PPA?
  • First as names: which poets get invoked as authorities.
  • Then as lines: the actual verse that gets quoted; the verses that are being cut into couplets and stanzas to do explanatory work.
  • In this presentation, I will present on those two dimensions of canonicity: first, how names are used to invoke poets as authorities; and second, exactly which poems are being quoted.

    A poet’s name is not a stable search object — so we resolve every mention to one identity.

  • The question is basically: who gets named when a versification wants to explain how verse works?
  • The problem: a poet’s name is a moving target — Shakespeare alone shows up spelled a dozen ways, plus ‘the bard’, or ‘the sweet swan of Avon.’
  • So we can't really do a simple text search, because then we might miss all of these variants, let alone artifacts of OCR.
  • What we did instead: we used a neural entity linker -- ReFinED -- that reads each mention in context [click] and ties it to one identity in Wikidata.
  • [Click.] Do that across the whole PPA and the names collapse into countable people
  • That gave us about 737,000 mentions, resolved to just over 3,000 poets.
  • [Accuracy and filtering are on the backup slide just below, if anyone asks.]

    • Method

    From name strings to poets

    Named-entity recognition only says “Shelley is a person.” Entity linking says which Shelley — Mary or Percy Bysshe — and pulls “Mrs. Shelley,” “Mary Wollstonecraft Shelley,” and the bare surname onto one identity across two centuries.

    • the linker ReFinED, zero-shot. A neural model that detects and disambiguates each mention jointly from context, mapping it to a Wikidata QID (Dante → Q1067) with a 0–1 confidence. No corpus-specific training; ~1 day on an A100 over 1.68M pages.
    • the Wikidata trap Linking is only as clean as the knowledge base. Wikidata files Friedrich Fröbel (the kindergarten founder) and Elizabeth Barrett Browning (d. 1861) among “soccer players” and “screenwriters” — so the poet set must be filtered, not taken raw.
    • how accurate? ~98% at the 0.90 threshold. On 50 hand-annotated pages (3 annotators), 56 of 57 high-confidence links were correct. The system errs toward silence: its misses are hard OCR forms (“Bobert Bums” → Burns, “THEOCRITUS”), not fabricated poets.
    • the cascade From spans to a poet universe. 23.9M mention spans → 11.6M linked → 92,823 humans → poet-flagged (Q49757) → conf. ≥0.90 → seen in ≥2 works → born ≤1900. Noise lives in singletons — the stability gate drops 34% of names but only 0.4% of mentions.

    Final poet universe: 3,164 poets · 737,194 mentions across 3,149 works, 1559–1929

  • The linker is ReFinED: neural, no training needed (though one can finetune!); it reads each name in context and picks the right Wikidata person.
  • We checked it by hand on fifty pages — about 98% correct at our confidence cutoff, and when it’s unsure it stays silent rather than inventing poets.
  • The funnel runs from 24 million raw name-spans down to about 3,000 poets.
  • The one trap: Wikidata sometimes mislabels people, so we don't take the raw 'poet' tag on faith.
  • That gives us our final poet universe: 3,164 poets and 737,194 mentions across 3,149 works, 1559–1929.

    • A superstar economy

    A handful of poets carry most of the attention.

    The top 1% — just 32 poets — account for 59% of all mentions (Gini 0.92). Shakespeare, Milton, Chaucer, Homer, Virgil at the head.

  • Attention here is wildly unequal. The top one percent of poets — just 32 names of those 3,164 poets — pull fifty-nine percent of all the mentions.
  • The top five percent (so roughly 160 poets), over eighty percent of the mentions.
  • A Gini of point-nine-two: about as concentrated as it gets. Gini is a measure of inequality; operationalized here as the degree to which mentions are distributed unevenly across poets. A Gini of zero would mean that every poet gets an equal number of mentions; a Gini of one would mean that all mentions belong to a single poet.
  • Of course, this is just one snapshot -- with all 3,164 poets in the pool.

    • A canon in motion

    The leaderboard reshuffles for two centuries — yet a few anchors never leave the head.

    Cumulative distinct works, by decade · top 10 · PPA Literary collection

  • As poets are born and begin writing, they start getting named in the PPA — and over the decades those mentions accumulate.
  • A bar-chart race (people who know me know there’s always one in my slides): poets ranked by the number of PPA works that cite them, decade by decade. The leaderboard keeps reshuffling as new PPA works appear and name poets.
  • But the churn happens inside a very stable cast. Homer and Virgil trade #1 and #2 for nearly two centuries — until Milton, climbing steadily from outside the top ten, and eventually overtakes them..
  • Shakespeare makes the same slow climb. No Romantics or Victorians crack this list at all — the cumulative example-canon stays classical and early-modern.
  • Chaucer is a very strong riser; mostly due to the Victorian rediscovery of the Middle Ages.
  • And poets don’t travel alone. This is a map -- a pretty big hairball actually -- of who gets mentioned alongside whom; and through community detection it has been sorted into neighborhoods: classical poets here, medieval there, etc.
  • I won’t dwell on it, but it’s a good reminder that a canon has a social shape, not just a ranking.

    • but a name isn’t a line

    Being named is not the same as having your words travel.

  • Now, being named is one thing. Having your actual words, lines travel and be used as examples to explain versification is another.
  • You can be invoked constantly without anyone quoting a single line of you.

  • A real example: an 1857 prosodist quotes the Elegy’s opening line — just that one line, buried inside his own sentence. How do you map a single embedded line back to a whole poem?

    • Method

    Finding quotations at scale

    A quotation is an asymmetric match: we align the PPA, where quotations surface, against a reference body of poetry — the Chadwyck-Healey English Poetry Database, deduplicated to 246,724 reference poems, plus Shakespeare’s plays, which Chadwyck-Healey leaves out.

    • the tool passim. Built for the Viral Texts project to find reprinted passages across noisy newspaper OCR. It needs no prior guess about which texts align, and is robust to OCR error and ragged passage boundaries.
    • tuned for verse Short quotes, not long reprints. passim’s defaults assume long documentary reuse, so we lowered the bar — n-gram 25→15, min length 50→25, gap 600→300, max DF 100→10,000 — with final values chosen against a hand-annotated gold set.
    • the unit Poem appearance, not raw record. OCR noise and paraphrase split one visible quotation into several alignment records, so we count a poem’s presence in a host work once — the level every question here operates at.
    • then filter Matches aren’t yet a corpus. Host-side (Literary/Linguistic collections, collapse reprints, 1694–1920) and poem-side (hand-remove centos & adaptations, a biographical-plausibility cut) filtering follow — the counts are the analytic-corpus detour one slide up.

    1,263,587 raw alignments → 509,030 appearances → 252,893 appearances of 37,406 poems across 3,962 host works

    The tool is passim, from the Viral Texts newspaper project — built for messy OCR, and it needs no prior guess about which texts match. We tuned it down for short verse quotations rather than long reprints, with the settings chosen against a hand-checked gold set. And we count a poem once per book it appears in, because OCR splits one quotation into several raw matches.

    A widening practice

    Distinct poems quoted per 100 pages of prosody writing — a rate, so it climbs only if quotation itself broadened, not merely because the archive grew.

  • Before we look at trajectories of individual poems, one big-picture fact.
  • This is how much poetry the PPA quotes over time -- but as a rate, per hundred pages, so it only rises if quoting genuinely broadened, not just because there were more books.
  • And it does rise: it roughly triples across the window, peaking around 1880.
  • [Click to mark 1774 — Donaldson v. Becket] An important moment here is the ruling in the case Donaldson v. Becket in 1774 that effectively created the public domain in Britain (after this ruling, copyright was no longer perpetual). Especially for older works, this meant it was safe to reprint them.
  • (The decision did not create quotation culture by itself, but it likely accelerated the process by making older poetry legally and commercially reusable.)

    • Method

    Holding the archive constant

    A raw count of distinct poems can’t separate a widening practice from a thickening archive — more host pages simply mean more chances to quote. So we model a rate, with the archive’s size netted out.

    \[ A(y) \sim \mathrm{NegBin}(\mu_y,\, \alpha_{\mathrm{disp}}) \qquad\quad \log \mu_y = \alpha + s(y) + \log P(y) \]
    • a rate, not a count The log‑pages offset. Page volume enters with its coefficient fixed at 1 — double the host pages, double the poems, by construction. The model is then free to find the departures from that proportionality.
    • a flexible trend A natural cubic spline s(y). Free to bend where the evidence calls for it; 7 degrees of freedom, chosen by AIC. “Natural” keeps the curve from flailing at the window’s edges.
    • room to wobble Negative Binomial, not Poisson. Yearly counts vary far more than their mean — one dense anthology spikes a year — so we relax the equal‑variance assumption. αdisp = 0.5; post‑fit dispersion ≈ 1.01.

    Modelled window 1694–1920  ·  1694 = first year with ≥5 PPA host works  ·  1920 = the PPA’s coverage horizon

  • We model a rate, poems per page, so archive growth divides out.
  • It’s a negative-binomial regression: a smooth spline for the trend over time; the page count entered as an offset, so ‘more pages’ can’t masquerade as ‘more quoting’; and the negative-binomial part absorbs the wild year-to-year spikes one fat anthology can cause.

    • A trajectory for every poem

    Which poems did prosodists reach for more over time — and which did they quietly set aside?

    \( \operatorname{logit}\,(p_{it}) = \) \( \alpha + s(t) \) shared time curve + \( b^{0}_{i} + b^{1}_{i}\, t \) poem slope + \( u_{a(i)} \) author shift

    \( p_{it} \) — a poem’s prevalence: the share of a decade’s prosody works that quote it.

    \( k_{it} \sim \text{Beta-Binomial}\,(n_t,\, p_{it}) \) — counts are overdispersed, so not a plain binomial.

    • Partial pooling. Thin-evidence poems are pulled toward the corpus trend; well-attested ones pull it.
    • Beta-binomial. Absorbs the superstar overdispersion a plain binomial can’t.
    • Eligibility window. Each poem is scored only in decades it could have been quoted (no left-truncation bias).

    Hierarchical Bayesian · 896 sustained-presence poems (≥30 host works across ≥10 decades)

  • The big question we’ve been building toward: quoting got more common across the board — but did the canon’s actual contents shift? Which poems gained ground, and which were quietly set aside?
  • We finally have all the cards in hand — every quotation located, dated, and tied to a poem — so we can give each poem its own trajectory.
  • The catch: if quoting broadened for everyone, a poem being quoted more isn’t necessarily gaining ground — it might just be riding the tide.
  • So we measure each poem against that shared rising tide.
  • [Build the formula.] A shared time curve everyone sits on; each poem’s own drift above or below it; and a small adjustment for how its author is doing in general.
  • The number we care about is that middle slope — each poem’s own b¹: is it gaining or losing ground relative to the field?
  • [ logit(pit) — we model on the log-odds scale, so the fitted prevalence always stays between 0 and 1. pit is the share of decade t’s prosody works that quote poem i. ]
  • [ α — the global baseline: the field’s overall quoting level, before any poem- or time-specific effect. ]
  • [ s(t) — the shared time curve: a smooth spline that bends with the overall rise and fall of quoting. The “rising tide” every poem sits on. ]
  • [ b0i — poem i’s own intercept: how heavily it’s quoted overall, relative to the field. ]
  • [ b1i t — poem i’s own slope through time, i.e. its trajectory. This b1 is the number we read off: positive = gaining ground, negative = fading. ]
  • [ ua(i) — an author-level shift: poems by the same author are pooled, so a single poem isn’t credited (or blamed) for its author’s general standing. ]
  • [ What makes it beta-binomial: the response, kit ~ Beta-Binomial(nt, pit). The Beta layer lets pit carry extra variance, soaking up the overdispersion a plain binomial can’t — a few superstar poems spike far more than binomial noise allows. ]

    • Risers, decliners, anchors

    Medieval and Romantic verse climbs; neoclassical translation fades; the perennials hold.

    And poems fall into three shapes. [Click.] Some rise — here Chaucer’s Canterbury Tales climbs steadily; the medieval revival again. [Click.] Some hold flat — Milton’s L’Allegro just keeps getting quoted, steady for two centuries. [Click.] And some fall — Virgil’s Aeneid, in Dryden’s translation, slides down and down. Risers, anchors, decliners. Let me show you who’s in each group.

    Method

    Reading each poem against the corpus

    \[ \operatorname{logit}\,(p_{it}) = \underbrace{\alpha + s(t)}_{\text{shared trend}} \;+\; \underbrace{b^{0}_{i} + b^{1}_{i}\,t}_{\text{poem deviation}} \;+\; \underbrace{u_{a(i)}}_{\text{author shift}} \]
    • the input Prevalence per decade. For each eligible poem, p̂ = kit/nt — the share of that decade’s PPA host works that quote it. Fitted with a beta‑binomial rather than a binomial, because the superstar poems are overdispersed.
    • the headline number The slope b¹ᵢ. Gaining or losing ground relative to the shared trend — not in absolute terms. A positive slope outpaces the average poem; a negative one lags even as the whole practice widens around it.
    • reading the slope Log‑odds per decade. +0.25 ≈ ×1.28 odds (~28% likelier to be quoted each decade); −0.20 ≈ ×0.82 (~18% less). “Credibly” rising/declining = the 95% credible interval on b¹ excludes zero.
    • keeping it honest Partial pooling + author shift. Thin‑evidence poems are pulled toward the corpus trend; well‑attested ones pull it. ua(i) separates a poem’s own movement from its author’s standing (Shakespeare: 37 works; Gray rests almost entirely on the Elegy).

    Eligibility starts 18 yrs after the poet’s birth — a Tennyson zero in the 1700s is impossibility, not neglect  ·  population = ≥30 host works across ≥10 decades → 896 poems  ·  Bambi / PyMC (NUTS), R̂ ≤ 1.02

    [Backup — if asked about the per-poem model.] For each poem in each decade we take its prevalence — the share of that decade’s books that quote it — and model it with a beta-binomial, which tolerates the superstars. The slope is the headline: gaining or losing relative to the shared trend. A slope of plus a quarter is roughly a 28% bump per decade in the odds of being quoted. Thin-evidence poems get pulled toward the average, and we only score a poem in decades it could actually have existed.

    Which eras gained ground

    And it isn’t just individual poems — it holds for whole eras. Each ridge is a literary period; left of the line means losing ground, right means gaining. [Reveal.] The Restoration and early-eighteenth-century cohorts sit clearly on the left — the neoclassical decline. Up top, leaning right, the medieval and Romantic periods. So the same story — away from neoclassical translation, toward the medieval and the Romantic — shows up at every scale: the line, the poem, the period.

    Method

    From poems to periods

    No new model — the same per‑poem slopes b¹ᵢ, re‑cut by the literary period each poem belongs to. Labels are Chadwyck‑Healey’s bibliographic categories (the database’s organizing logic).

    • why a period can surprise A period is a different unit from poems or authors. It can rise even when most of its authors are flat (a few rise sharply), or fall even when most of its poems hold (the high‑volume ones decline). The ridge checks whether the stories aggregate.
    • what’s included Only periods with ≥20 modelled poems. The catch‑all “Poems & Miscellanies, 1500–1900” — for poems that fit no named period — is excluded.
    • inside the Restoration ridge It’s bimodal: the classical‑translation and didactic‑verse apparatus fell hard (≈ −0.15), while Dryden’s original verse declined only mildly (≈ −0.05, intervals straddling zero).
    • inside Jacobean / Caroline Layered: short lyric and song rose, the Shakespeare plays and Milton’s longer poems held, the heroic‑narrative tradition gave way — lyricization in miniature.

    Slopes in log‑odds per decade vs the shared trend  ·  Middle English sits furthest right — Chaucer and the founders the nineteenth century canonized

    [Backup — if asked about the period grouping.] No new model — these are the same per-poem slopes, just grouped by the literary period each poem is labeled with in Chadwyck-Healey. A period can move even when its individual authors don’t, so it’s a useful cross-check. One nice detail: the Restoration ridge is actually two humps — the translation machinery falls hard, while Dryden’s own original verse only drifts down gently.
